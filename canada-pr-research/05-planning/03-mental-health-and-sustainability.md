# Mental Health and Sustainability

## Purpose
This note keeps mental health as a core planning variable in migration decisions, not an afterthought.

## Working Principle
- A plan that is technically possible but mentally unsustainable is not a good plan

## Main Risks During Migration
- Isolation
- Loss of routine
- Financial pressure
- Repeated rejection during job search
- English communication stress
- Cold climate and low-energy periods

## Signs My Condition May Be Worsening
- Inability to manage basic planning tasks
- Strong avoidance of all migration-related decisions
- Sleep disruption or major fatigue increase
- Noticeable hopelessness after setbacks
- Loss of ability to maintain daily structure

## Protective Measures
- Keep plans simple and staged
- Avoid large irreversible decisions without review checkpoints
- Use written checklists instead of relying on mood or memory
- Keep emergency financial reserve intact
- Maintain contact with trusted people where possible
- Reduce isolation risk in location and housing choices

## Planning Principles For Low-Energy Periods
- Prefer small next actions over broad strategy work
- Use the dashboard and inbox as low-friction entry points
- Limit active research topics at one time
- Reuse templates instead of starting from blank pages
- Pause decisions when thinking becomes chaotic

## Why Migration Planning Must Not Ignore Mental Health
- Depression can distort risk tolerance, motivation, and confidence
- A move can worsen instability if done without structure
- A sustainable plan should reduce chaos, not increase it

## Decision Triggers
- Go only if:
  - The plan is clear enough to reduce avoidable stress
- Delay if:
  - Current mental state is too unstable for transition
- Stop if:
  - Migration planning is causing serious deterioration without a realistic path forward
