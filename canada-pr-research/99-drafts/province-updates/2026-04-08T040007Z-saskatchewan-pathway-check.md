## What Changed
- No new Saskatchewan pathway or eligibility change was posted on the official pages checked during this 2026-04-08 run.
- The most recent active rule set still in force is the April 1, 2026 expansion of the SINP fee to all worker applicants: $500 application fee and $250 second-review fee.
- Saskatchewan continues to use the 2026 sector-allocation model shown in the FAQs and processing pages: minimum 50 per cent for priority sectors, maximum 25 per cent for capped sectors, and 750 reserved spots for Saskatchewan post-secondary graduates in priority sectors.
- Capped-sector controls remain active: accommodation/food service, retail, and trucking use scheduled intake windows and require the candidate to have six months or less left on the work permit at submission time.

## What Did Not Change
- No new reopening of a broad low-barrier overseas pathway was posted. Saskatchewan still centers employer-linked pathways and sector priorities.
- Occupation In-Demand and Saskatchewan Express Entry remain weak options for this profile because Saskatchewan continues to prioritize employer-linked routes and maintains occupation restrictions.
- Priority sectors remain healthcare, agriculture, skilled trades, energy, mining, manufacturing, and technology.
- PGWP and open-work-permit access did not broaden in this check. PGWP holders are still limited to specific sub-categories, and open spousal work permit holders remain ineligible for the Existing Work Permit category.
- Processing targets shown on the official processing page did not indicate a slowdown severe enough to change feasibility on their own. Quarter 1, 2026 posted times remain short for most worker streams.

## Relevance To My Situation
- Saskatchewan still looks much more realistic if I first secure a Saskatchewan employer and work in a priority or at least non-capped sector. It does not look friendly for a passive overseas application without a job offer.
- If my likely survival-job path is food service, retail, or trucking, Saskatchewan is less practical because those sectors are capped, intake-window-based, and restricted to workers already nearing permit expiry.
- If I end up on an open spousal work permit, Saskatchewan remains less flexible than some provinces because that permit type is blocked from the Existing Work Permit stream.
- If I can target tech, healthcare-adjacent, agriculture, manufacturing, or another priority-sector employer, Saskatchewan remains more viable than it would be for a generic low-skill job search.

- The official pages do not clearly signal whether more 2026 reallocations will happen later in the year beyond the stated mid-year review.
