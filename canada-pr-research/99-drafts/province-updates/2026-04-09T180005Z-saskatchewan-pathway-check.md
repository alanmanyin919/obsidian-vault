## What Changed
- Saskatchewan is now operating under a 2026 SINP allocation of 4,761 nominations. The 2025 federal rule that required 75 per cent of nominees to already be temporary residents in Canada is no longer listed in the 2026 FAQ.
- Effective 2026-04-01, Saskatchewan extended the $500 application fee and $250 second-review fee to all worker applicants in all categories.
- Saskatchewan’s 2026 sector model is now clearly posted across SINP pages: at least 50 per cent of nominations are reserved for priority sectors, with current priority sectors listed as healthcare, agriculture, skilled trades, mining, manufacturing, energy, and technology.
- Capped sectors remain tightly controlled in 2026: accommodation and food services, trucking, and retail trade together can use at most 25 per cent of nominations, and employers in those sectors can submit Job Approval Forms only during six intake windows and only for workers with six months or less left on their work permits.
- The 2026 FAQ adds that 750 nomination spots within priority sectors are reserved for Saskatchewan post-secondary graduates working in those sectors.

## What Did Not Change
- Saskatchewan is still strongly employer-driven. For most realistic routes, a Saskatchewan employer still needs to obtain a Job Approval Letter before the worker applies.
- The no-job-offer route is still weak in practice. The EOI system page says there are no scheduled EOI draws at this time, even though the EOI pool still exists for Occupations In-Demand and Express Entry.
- Occupation In-Demand and Express Entry still require high-skilled work experience tied to an eligible occupation, and occupation exclusions still apply.
- Open spousal work permit holders are still not eligible for the Saskatchewan Experience Existing Work Permit category. Their possible Saskatchewan options remain narrower, mainly Employment Offer, Health Talent, Agriculture Talent, or Tech Talent if they otherwise qualify.
- I did not find a new April 2026 Saskatchewan government news release announcing another major SINP pathway redesign beyond the already-posted 2026 rules and fee change.

## Relevance To My Situation
- For a profile centered on lower-barrier office support, admin support, service desk, or application support work, Saskatchewan still looks hard unless there is a real employer-backed job offer and the role fits a high-skilled occupation or a technology-sector pathway.
- Saskatchewan is more favorable than the 2025 version in one narrow sense: the province no longer shows the 75 per cent in-Canada nominee requirement in its 2026 overview. But this does not make Saskatchewan broadly easy, because the province is still prioritizing specific sectors and still relies heavily on employer sponsorship mechanics.
- If the realistic path is an open work permit plus in-Canada work, Saskatchewan may still be usable, but the strongest route appears to be employer-linked Employment Offer or, if the role truly qualifies, Tech Talent. General no-job-offer planning remains weak.
- If the realistic target role is outside priority sectors, Saskatchewan looks less attractive than provinces or pathways that are less dependent on a Saskatchewan employer and Job Approval Letter.

## Uncertainties
- Saskatchewan’s official pages show current 2026 rules, but I did not confirm whether any additional mid-year nomination allocation changes have happened after the posted 4,761 figure.
- The practical fit depends heavily on the exact target NOC code. A role described loosely as IT support, application support, or office support may or may not fit a qualifying high-skilled occupation or the technology-sector route.
- The EOI system page still says there are no scheduled draws, but Saskatchewan could change draw activity later without much notice.
